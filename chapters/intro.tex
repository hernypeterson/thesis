\chapter{Introduction}

\ac{OM} is the largest volcano in the solar system (or anywhere else known to sicence), with a diameter of roughly \qty{600}{\km} and a summit over \qty{20}{\km}~\parencite{plescia_morphometric_2004}. See Figure~\ref{om}. The striking observation which catalyzed the current inquiry is that numerous lava flows around its summit caldera appear to flow uphill~\parencite{mouginis-mark_late-stage_2019}. See Figure~\ref{uphill-flows}. Similar observations have been made in the flexural basin surrounding the main edifice, although these horiontal misalignments between flow direction and regional dip direction are up to \ang{\sim15}~\parencite{chadwick_late_2015}, rather than the \ang{\sim180} deflections observed at the summit. In both cases, however, these discrepancies suggest a history of surface deformation that occured \textit{after} the lava flows were emplaced in what was once a downhill direction. The aim of the current projct is to quantify this surface deformation and describe the underlying processes responsible.

\section{Challenges \& Assumptions}
Volcanic processes implicated by inferred surface deformation include combination of a) caldera collapse (i.e., magmatic discharge) and b) subsequent reservoir inflation (i.e., magmatic recharge). \section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Geologic Overview of Martian Volcanism}
The volcanic history of Mars differs from that of the Earth in many ways. On Earth, dominant volcanic systems include mid-ocean ridges, island \& continental arcs, hotspot chains, and \acp{LIP}. Ridges and arcs have no clear analogues on Mars; hotspots and \acp{LIP} share many similaries with martian features but reflect differing thermal, mechanical, and chemical properties.

The martian geologic timescale\footnote{It is interesting to note the large-scale differences reflected in the respective geologic timescales used for each planet. On Earth, finer temporal gradations exist for more recent history where rock and fossil evidence is more abundant. On Mars, it is the earlier two systems which saw the largest-scale changes inferred by modern evidence. Nonetheless, there is considerablr evidence for volcanic activity during longer and ``quieter'' Amazonian system.} is different from the terrestrial one. The most precise absolute dating methods using radioisotopes are largely unavailable because few martian samples exist on Earth. Nonetheless, radiochronometry of  martian meteorites has revealed mid-Amazonian volcanic eruptions,\cite{cohen_taking_2017} etc. Instead, researchers count craters on surfaces and examine stratigraphic relationships. From these techniques, three major periods emerge. First, the Noachian period lasted roughly from \qtyrange{4.1}{3.7}{Ga}.\cite{carr_geologic_2010} During this period, volcanic activity was common everywhere on Mars. The Tharsis Plateau and \ac{OM} edifices had been largely constructed by the end of the Noachian.

Next, the Hesperian period lasted between 3.7 to 3.1? Ga. End of late heavy bombardment, less water (although still some flooding) and eruptions begin to concentrate within the Tharsis and Elysium volcanic provinces.

Finally, the Amazonian period is ongoing today from 3.1? Ga. It has been characterized by reduced volcanism localized in the major provinces, limited fluvial activity, and slow sedimentary processes.

\section{Planetary Volcanism Perspective}
The study of \ac{OM} contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of magma reservoirs than could be achieved with a solely Earth-based inquiry. Compared to Earth, the unique challenges of studying martian volcanoes are obvious: the inability to make in-situ measurements and the lack of evidence for ongoing eruptive activity stand out. However, there are important advantages as well. Most notably, surface deformation signatures are well preserved in the martian environment over hundreds of millions of years, especially at high elevations where weathering and erosion are far less active. This allows for a more comprehensive picture of edifice deformation than is commonly available on Earth, where such edifices are rare and often poorly preserved. Additionally, the unique martian conditions that yield the largest volcanoes in the solar system broaden our perspective of how volcanism works in general. Finally, previous work investigating \ac{OM} and nearby Tharsis Montes has already yielded clear evidence for surface deformation. Specifically, numerous lava flows (which generally move straight downhill during eruptions) are misaligned with the local topographic downhill direction measured by the \ac{MOLA}. This finding suggests subsequent deformation, most likely because of reservoir activity at summit of \ac{OM}. The current project’s role is to provide a quantitative link between observed surface deformation and inferred volcanic activity.
