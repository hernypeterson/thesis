\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Motivation}
The study of \ac{OM} contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of magma reservoirs than could be achieved with a solely Earth-based inquiry. Compared to Earth, the unique challenges of studying martian volcanoes are obvious: the inability to make in-situ measurements and the lack of evidence for ongoing eruptive activity stand out. However, there are important advantages as well. Most notably, surface deformation signatures are well preserved in the martian environment over hundreds of millions of years, especially at high elevations where weathering and erosion are far less active. This allows for a more comprehensive picture of edifice deformation than is commonly available on Earth, where such edifices are rare and often poorly preserved. Additionally, the unique martian conditions that yield the largest volcanoes in the solar system broaden our perspective of how volcanism works in general. Finally, previous work investigating Olympus Mons and nearby Tharsis Montes has already yielded clear evidence for surface deformation. Specifically, numerous lava flows (which generally move straight downhill during eruptions) are misaligned with the local topographic downhill direction measured by the \ac{MOLA}. This finding suggests subsequent deformation, most likely because of reservoir activity at summit of Olympus Mons. The current project’s role is to provide a quantitative link between observed surface deformation and inferred reservoir activity.

\section{Research Objectives}
The following question will guide this research project: what combination of magma reservoir characteristics (e.g., geometry, depth) and magma injection event parameters best accounts for both \begin{enumerate}
    \item observed lava flow orientations
    \item modern summit topography
\end{enumerate} My hypothesis is that significant pressurization of a shallow, relatively small-footprint magma reservoir centered near the modern summit will best explain the observed discrepancy between the observed surface and the older deflected lava flows. To test this hypothesis, I will \begin{enumerate}
    \item map long lava flows near the surface
    \item constrain their deflection from the modern topographic surface, i.e.\ the extent to which they are not flowing ‘downhill’
    \item use crater dating to estimate relative flow ages to constrain a timeline of deformation
    \item run numerical models of the volcano and summit reservoir to assess the conditions needed to generate observed topographic deflections.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Geologic Overview of Mars \& \ac{OM}}
The volcanic history of Mars differs from that of the Earth in many ways. On Earth, dominant volcanic systems include mid-ocean ridges, island \& continental arcs, hotspot chains, and \acp{LIP}. Ridges and arcs have no clear analogues on Mars; hotspots and \acp{LIP} share many similaries with martian features but reflect differing thermal, mechanical, and chemical properties.
