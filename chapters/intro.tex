\section{Motivation}
The study of \ac{OM} contributes to a more comprehensive understanding of magma reservoirs than could be achieved with a solely Earth-based inquiry. Compared to Earth, the unique challenges of studying martian volcanoes are obvious: the inability to make in-situ measurements and the lack of evidence for ongoing eruptive activity stand out. However, there are important advantages as well. Most notably, surface deformation signatures are well preserved in the martian environment over hundreds of millions of years, especially at high elevations where weathering and erosion are far less active. This allows for a more comprehensive picture of edifice deformation than is commonly available on Earth, where such edifices are rare and often poorly preserved. Additionally, the unique martian conditions that yield the largest volcanoes in the solar system broaden our perspective of how volcanism works in general. Finally, previous work investigating \ac{OM} and nearby Tharsis Montes has already yielded clear evidence for surface deformation. Specifically, numerous lava flows (which generally move straight downhill during eruptions) are misaligned with the local topographic downhill direction measured by the \ac{MOLA}. This finding suggests subsequent deformation, most likely because of reservoir activity at summit of \ac{OM}. The current project’s role is to provide a quantitative link between observed surface deformation and inferred reservoir activity.

\section{Research Objectives}
The following question will guide this research project: what combination of magma reservoir characteristics (e.g., geometry, depth) and magma injection event parameters best accounts for both \begin{enumerate}
    \item observed lava flow orientations
    \item modern summit topography
\end{enumerate} My hypothesis is that significant pressurization of a shallow, relatively small-footprint magma reservoir centered near the modern summit will best explain the observed discrepancy between the observed surface and the older deflected lava flows. To test this hypothesis, I will \begin{enumerate}
    \item map long lava flows near the surface
    \item constrain their deflection from the modern topographic surface, i.e.\ the extent to which they are not flowing ‘downhill’
    \item use crater dating to estimate relative flow ages to constrain a timeline of deformation
    \item run numerical models of the volcano and summit reservoir to assess the conditions needed to generate observed topographic deflections.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Geologic Overview of Martian Volcanism}
The volcanic history of Mars differs from that of the Earth in many ways. On Earth, dominant volcanic systems include mid-ocean ridges, island \& continental arcs, hotspot chains, and \acp{LIP}. Ridges and arcs have no clear analogues on Mars; hotspots and \acp{LIP} share many similaries with martian features but reflect differing thermal, mechanical, and chemical properties.

The martian geologic timescale\footnote{It is interesting to note the large-scale differences reflected in the respective geologic timescales used for each planet. On Earth, finer temporal gradations exist for more recent history where rock and fossil evidence is more abundant. On Mars, it is the earlier two systems which saw the largest-scale changes inferred by modern evidence. Nonetheless, there is considerablr evidence for volcanic activity during longer and ``quieter'' Amazonian system.} is different from the terrestrial one. The most precise absolute dating methods using radioisotopes are largely unavailable because few martian samples exist on Earth. Nonetheless, radiochronometry of  martian meteorites has revealed mid-Amazonian volcanic eruptions,\cite{cohen_taking_2017} etc. Instead, researchers count craters on surfaces and examine stratigraphic relationships. From these techniques, three major periods emerge. First, the Noachian period lasted roughly from \qtyrange{4.1}{3.7}{Ga}.\cite{carr_geologic_2010} During this period, volcanic activity was common everywhere on Mars. The Tharsis Plateau and \ac{OM} edifices had been largely constructed by the end of the Noachian.

Next, the Hesperian period lasted between 3.7 to 3.1? Ga. End of late heavy bombardment, less water (although still some flooding) and eruptions begin to concentrate within the Tharsis and Elysium volcanic provinces.

Finally, the Amazonian period is ongoing today from 3.1? Ga. It has been characterized by reduced volcanism localized in the major provinces, limited fluvial activity, and slow sedimentary processes.

\subsection{The Lava Flow Alignment Method}
Regardless of their locations, flow orientations are identified primarily by distinct lobate ``toes'' and nearby parallel flow margins (reword). In many cases, source vents and nearby margins have been obscured by additional flows or caldera collape. Additionally, there are several central lava channels whose corresponding margins are not discernable. Some of these channels are continuous, while some are interpolated between linear sequences of cavities that resemble lava tube collapse skylights.
