\chapter{Methods}
\section{Overview}
Goal (a) of this project is to constrain the degree and character of 3D surface deformation recorded at the summit of \ac{OM} to the greatest spatial extent possible. 3D deformation is a characterization of net change; thus, initial and final surface attitude conditions are necessary. Attitude is completely defined by two angles; strike and dip are most widely used in geology. In this inquiry, dip direction will be used instead of strike because the lava flows naturally point in the dip direction of the surface upon which they are emplaced. No assumptions are made \textit{a priori} about large-scale spatial patterns in 3D deformation; therefore, independent calculations will be made at all locations where sufficient information is available.

Goal (b) of the project is to estimate magma reservoir inflation conditions (location, depth, size of reservoir, degree of inflation) consistent with observed 3D deformation at the surface. This process will entail running a series of numerical models simulating various possible combinations of magma reservoir conditions, analyzing the resultant surface deformation associated with each, and identifying the best match with the observed and calculated surface parameters.

While the technical definitions of these surface parameters will be presented later in Section~\ref{paleosurface}, it is worth noting here that 3D deformation discussed previously is a scalar term: in particular, the angular distance between the two normal vectors corresponding to each of the initial and final surfaces. This parameter plays two key roles in this project.

First, relevant to goal (a), it builds on the qualitative observations of \textcite{mouginis-mark_late-stage_2019} and extends the quantitative horizontal description of flow misalignment in \textcite{chadwick_late_2015} by accounting for vertical deformation immediately as a central component of the map-derived data collection.\footnote{Vertical deformation is accounted for in the geophysical modeling stage of \textcite{chadwick_late_2015}.} In other words, constraints on 3D deformation may be computed directly from the \ac{DEM} and mapped lava flows \& channels, prior to running numerical models. This strategy gives first-order insights by mapping regions where especially high magnitudes of 3D deformation are required to explain observed surface features. 

However, the 3D deformation parameter cannot simply be calculated for each point because insufficient attitude information is available for the paleosurface upon which lava flows and channels were originally emplaced. Recall that 3D deformation is the angular distance between the two normal vectors defining each of the initial and final surfaces. Each normal vector (equivalently, each surface attitude) requires two variables: dip and dip direction. Both of these are available for the modern (final) surface \ac{DEM}; however, only dip direction of the paleosurface can be inferred directly from the direction of lava flows. Rather than attempting to estimate paleoslope (dip) from lava flow morphology, this parameter is \textit{constrained.} Specifically, the paleo-dip assigned to each point answers the question: \textit{given modern dip, modern dip direction, and paleo-dip direction, what paleo-dip minimizes 3D deformation?} Through the simplifying assumption of minimum 3D deformation, two variable constraints (paleo-dip \& 3D deformation) can be obtained for the price of one, so to speak.

Second, relevant to goal (b), the previous discussion has shown how an assumption regarding of 3D deformation yields an estimate of paleo-dip which is independent of lava flow morphology (and can thus be compared with estimates derived from such methods). This is important because the numerical models used to test magma reservoir inflation events require construction of a paleo-edifice which no longer exists, and whose geometry must thus be inferred. Therefore, estimates of paleo-dip in addition to measurements of paleo-dip direction will guide first attempts at constructing this paleo-edifice.

To summarize, the 3D deformation strategy is to \begin{inparaenum}[a)]
    \item identify suitable point locations across the summit,
    \item collect attitude information for the modern surface at each point,
    \item infer paleoattitude information based on lava flows or channels at each point, and
    \item constrain total 3D deformation based on initial and final surface attitudes at each point. In this final step, an estimate of paleo-dip is produced as a additional useful result.
\end{inparaenum}
The following sections describe the details of each of these steps.

\section{Selection and Preparation of Published Data}
The \ac{CTX}\footnote{aboard the \ac{MRO} spacecraft launched in 2005 by \ac{NASA}.} captures \qty{\sim30}{\km} swaths across the entire martian surface in visible greyscale at \qty{\sim6}{\m} resolution. \textcite{Dickson2018AGB} blended these swaths to produce a raster mosaic product (hereafter, ``\ac{CTX} mosaic'') which is used here to visually identify lava flows and flow channels. In other words, this is the source from which paleo-dip direction is estimated across the summit region.

\ac{MOLA}\footnote{aboard the now-retired \ac{MGS} spacecraft launched in 1996 by \ac{NASA}.} returned topography data with horizontal resolution of \qtyproduct{300 x 1000}{\m} at the equator (better at high latitudes) and elevation uncertainty of \qty{\sim3}{\m}. To improve spatial resolution, additional elevation data from \ac{HRSC}\footnote{aboard the \ac{MEX} spacecraft launched in 2003 by \ac{ESA}} was blended to product a \ac{DEM} with \qty{200}{\m} pixel resolution. Each pixel's vertical uncertainty is \qty{\sim1}{\m}, with an additional global uncertainty of \qty{\sim1.8}{\m} in the martian areoid (martian equivalent of Earth's geoid). In this project, the global areoid uncertainty is not a concern because only one region (the summit of \ac{OM}) is considered.

These two data sources were registered in an equal-area sinusoidal Mars projection in ArcGIS Pro. The study area is defined by a square \qtyproduct{200 x 200}{\km} centered at the centroid of the \qty{19}{\km} contour.\footnote{This is the highest integer \unit{km} which is roughly circular and completely encloses the caldera complex, implying that it largely records the conical shape of the shield edifice without influence from subsequent caldera collapse or reservoir inflation.} See Figure~\ref{study-area}.

In both cases, lava features are ultimately mapped as lines\footnote{Lines can have more than one vertex, i.e., they can be curved.} (rather than polygons) in ArcGIS Pro. While the \ac{DEM} covers the entire summit region, it is only along these lines that information about the paleosurface can be inferred. Therefore, points along these lines are selected as sampling locations where attitude measurements and calculations will take place. Points are produced at evenly spaced intervals of \qty{5}{\km} along each line. No points are assigned to linear features of less than this length to ensure that flows and channels are long enough to record large-scale topography rather than local variation.

\section{Modern Surface (Final) Parameters}
The ``Surface Parameters'' tool was used in ArcGIS Pro to compute average dip $\theta$ and dip direction $\varphi$ across the entire study area. In both cases, a new raster is produced at the same spatial resolution and coverage as the original \ac{DEM}. To avoid capturing local topographic anomalies, these values are averaged\footnote{Technically, a quadratic surface is interpolated over the neighborhood region and dip and dip direction are computed at the center of this surface.} over a circular ``neighborhood'' with radius \qty{2}{\km}. To each sampling location identified in Section~\ref{mapping} is assigned dip $\theta$ and dip direction $\varphi$ based on the value of the corresponding raster at that location.

\section{Paleosurface (Initial) Parameters}\label{paleosurface}
\subsection{Paleo-Dip Direction}
Paleo-dip direction $\theta'$ is assigned to each point directly from the linear lava feature from which the point was sampled. Therefore, it is important to ensure that each feature is oriented with the correct sense, i.e., pointing downhill rather than uphill. Longer features with noticeable large-scale bends are broken into multiple pieces which are approximately straight. (Goal aspect ratio?) Then, the primary dip direction is calculated for each feature and assigned to all points sampled within it.

It is not possible to assign a paleo-dip $\varphi'$ directly from the lava flows as it is with paleo-dip direction $\theta'$. However, in the following discussion it will become clear that calculated 3D deformation $D$ depends on all four variables: $\theta,\varphi,\theta',\varphi'$ which completely define each of the initial and final surface attitudes. However, this calculation can be reframed as an optimization problem to obtain constraints on $D$ and $\varphi'$ simultaneously.

The strategy for solving this problem is as follows. First, each surface attitude is interpreted as a unit normal vector expressed in 3D polar coordinates. Since all normal vectors are of unit length, surfaces are interpreted as points on the surface of a sphere, which will prove to be a useful model. The dip of a surface is equal to the colatitude of its normal vector; the dip direction of a surface is equal to the longitude of its normal vector. Since both dip and dip direction are known for the modern (final) surface at each point, the surface attitude is represented by a point on the imaginary unit sphere. Since only dip direction is known of the paleosurface of lava flow/channel emplacement, the range of possible surfaces is represented by an entire line of longitude on the unit sphere. In light of this model, 3D deformation $D$ between two surface attitudes is defined as the (great-circle) distance between their respective point representations on the unit sphere.

Some point withing the line of longitude representing the family of possible paleo-dip values represents the true dip, dip-direction pair at the time of flow emplacement. Which point along this line minimizes the angular distance between the (paleosurface) line and the (modern surface) point? In other words, what paleo-dip $\varphi'$ minimizes total 3D deformation $D$?

This problem is trivial in 2D cartesian coordinates, so the following section outlines the mathematical procedure used to convert the spherical polar case into a planar cartesian analog that retains the key properties of the problem. In particular, the spherical surface should be projected onto a plane such that shortest-paths are preserved; great circle arcs should map to straight lines.

The gnomonic projection has this property. See Figure~\ref{gnomonic}.

The procedure to solve for $\varphi'$ follow. First, note that the gnomonic projection does not affect longitude $\theta$, so the same variable symbol is used in 3D and 2D polar coordinates. Thus the line\footnote{This line is really a ray from the origin, because any negative values of $r$ for a given $\theta$ in polar coordinates will correspond to a negative slope, which would have been captured by a positive slope for the opposite aspect. This case will be handled later.} of longitude corresponding to the paleo-dip direction does not change when projected into 2D polar coordinates $(\theta'=\theta')$. In 2D cartesian coordinates, this line has the form:

\begin{equation}
    y=x\cot \theta'.\label{line}
\end{equation}

The point corresponding to the modern surface in polar coordinates:

\begin{equation}
    [r,\theta] = [\tan \varphi, \theta].
\end{equation}

In cartesian coordinates:

\begin{equation}
    [x,y]=[\tan\varphi\sin\theta,\tan\varphi\cos\theta].\label{point}
\end{equation}

Recall that the goal is to identify the shortest path (minimum 3D deformation $D$) between the longitudinal line on the sphere (reflecting paleoaspect with unknown paleoslope) and the point (reflecting known moderns slope and aspect). The gnomonic projection was used because great-circle (shortest distance) paths on a sphere are projected as straight lines. Therefore, we need to find the shortest path between the cartesian line and the cartesian point. The point where this path intersects the line will then place a constraint on paleoslope; i.e., it answers the question ``which paleoslope ($s'$) yields the points [$s',a'$] given a known $a'$ which is closest to [$s,a$].'' The closest path from the point to the line is perpendicular to the line. Perpendicular to slope $m=\cot a'$ is slope $m=-\tan a'$. The full point-slope form of the line is:
\begin{equation}
    y-\tan s \cos a=-\tan a'(x-\tan s\sin a).\label{perp}
\end{equation}
We are looking for the intersection point of these two lines, the point that satisfies both~\ref{line} and~\ref{perp}. Substituting from~\ref{line} to find the $x$-coordinate of the intersection point: 
\begin{gather}
    x\cot a'-\tan s \cos a=-\tan a'(x-\tan s\sin a)\nonumber\\
    x=\frac{\tan s(\cos a+\tan a'\sin a)}{\cot a' + \tan a'}\label{x}
\end{gather}
Since the math module in python does not include cotangent, I used the following exact formula in ArcGIS, which is equivalent to~\ref{x}:
\begin{equation}
    x=\sin(2a')\tan s(\cos a+\tan a'\sin a)/2
\end{equation}
And from~\ref{line}, the $y-$coordinate of the intersection point:
\begin{equation}
    y=x\cot a'
\end{equation}
The polar coordinate $r$ is given by $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$; which is reprojected back onto the sphere, giving the paleoslope:
\begin{equation}
    s'=\arctan\sqrt{x^2+y^2}
\end{equation}
The final result of this sequence of calculations is to identify the paleoslope which, having been deformed to give the modern surface, would have required the least possible angular change in attitude.
\subsection{Deflection}
Before proceeding, we should check that the deflection (defined $A-A'$) does not have a magnitude greater than \ang{90}. If it does, then this calculation has produced a nearest uphill slope, rather than downhill slope, which is clearly not the desired behavior. Therefore, at this stage any grid points with such deflections have their paleoslopes redefined to zero. Alternatively, a minimum slope required for lava flow can also be set here.
\subsection{Deformation}
From~\ref{paleo-dip} we have generated two points on a spherical surface, each corresponding to a unit vector normal to a unique surface. The angular distance between these vectors (also, the angle between the two planes) defines the attribute ``DEFORM'' for each grid point. The formula\footnote{The trigonometric functions cos and sin are swapped for $s$ and $s'$ because these are colatitudes, not latitudes as the most common formula assumes.} is:
\begin{equation}
    \arccos\left[\cos s \cos s' + \sin s \sin s' \cos(a - a')\right] 
\end{equation}
converted into degrees for comparison with other attributes.
