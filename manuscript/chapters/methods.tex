% \section{Overview}
I first sample paleo- and modern surface attitude data across the summit of \acl{OM}. Next, I develop a geometric framework for calculating minimum 3D surface deformation at each sampled location, based on collected attitude data. Next, I answer the question \emph{how much of this deformation must have been directly toward or away from the center of the edifice?} for each sampled point. Using these results, I develop a series of axisymmetric numerical models to relate surface deformation to underlying reservoir activity.

\section{Preparation of Published Data}
The \ac{CTX}\footnote{aboard the \ac{MRO} spacecraft launched in 2005 by \ac{NASA}.} captures \qty{\sim30}{\km} swaths across the entire martian surface in visible greyscale at \qty{\sim6}{\m} resolution. \textcite{Dickson2018AGB} blended these swaths to produce a raster mosaic product (hereafter, ``\ac{CTX} mosaic'') which is used here to visually identify lava flows and flow channels. In other words, this is the source from which paleo-dip direction is estimated across the summit region.

\ac{MOLA}\footnote{aboard the now-retired \ac{MGS} spacecraft launched in 1996 by \ac{NASA}.} returned topography data with horizontal resolution of \qtyproduct{300 x 1000}{\m} at the equator (better at high latitudes) and elevation uncertainty of \qty{\sim3}{\m}~\parencite{smith_mars_2001}. To improve spatial resolution, additional elevation data from the \ac{HRSC}\footnote{aboard the \ac{MEX} spacecraft launched in 2003 by the \ac{ESA}} was blended to product a \ac{DEM} with \qty{200}{\m} pixel resolution. Each pixel's vertical uncertainty is \qty{\sim1}{\m}, with an additional global uncertainty of \qty{\sim1.8}{\m} in the martian areoid (martian equivalent of Earth's geoid). In this project, the global areoid uncertainty is not a concern because only one region (the summit of \ac{OM}) is considered.

These two data sources were registered in an equal-area sinusoidal Mars projection in ArcGIS Pro. The study area is defined by a square \qtyproduct{200 x 200}{\km} centered at the centroid of the \qty{19}{\km} contour,\footnote{This is the highest integer \unit{km} which is roughly circular and completely encloses the caldera complex, implying that it largely records the conical shape of the shield edifice without influence from subsequent caldera collapse or reservoir inflation.} as seen in Figure~\ref{fig:study-area}.

In both cases, lava features are ultimately mapped as lines\footnote{Lines can have more than one vertex, i.e., they can be curved.} (rather than polygons) in ArcGIS Pro. While the \ac{DEM} covers the entire summit region, it is only along these lines that information about the paleosurface can be inferred. Therefore, points along these lines are selected as sampling locations where attitude measurements and calculations will take place. Points are produced at evenly spaced intervals of \qty{5}{\km} along each flow centerline and \qty{3}{\km} along each channel. No points are assigned to linear features of less than this length to ensure that flows and channels are long enough to record large-scale topography rather than local variation.

\section{Attitude Data Collection \& Representation}
The ``Surface Parameters'' tool was used in ArcGIS Pro to compute average \acf{ze} and \acf{az} across the entire study area. In both cases, a new raster is produced at the same spatial resolution and coverage as the original \ac{DEM}. To avoid capturing local topographic anomalies, these values are averaged\footnote{Technically, a quadratic surface is interpolated over the neighborhood region and dip and dip direction are computed at the center of this surface.} over a circular ``neighborhood'' with radius \qty{2}{\km}. To each sampling location identified in Section~\ref{mapping} is assigned \acf{ze} and \acf{az} based on the value of the corresponding raster at that location. Figure~\ref{geometric-deform-collected} shows a geometric view of the relevant data to be collected.

\subsection{Minimum 3D Deformation}\label{3d-deform}
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \input{figures/geometric-surface.tex}
    \caption[\Acl{az}-\acl{ze} representation of surfaces.]{The attitude of a surface is defined by two angles. In geology, strike and dip are the most common. In this thesis, slope (equivalent to dip) and downhill azimuth (\ang{90} from strike, equal to ``dip direction'') are used. For the mathematical procedures that follow, it will be convenient to describe surfaces by their normal (perpendicular) direction. In particular, unit vectors will be described in spherical coordinates: \acf{az} and \acf{ze}.}
\label{geometric-surface}
\end{figure}
Figure~\ref{geometric-deform-calculated} shows a geometric view of the relevant data to be calculated and a conceptual model of the method used.
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \input{figures/geometric-deform.tex}
    \caption{Geometric view of optimization method}\label{geometric-deform}
\end{figure}
By the spherical law of cosines, the \acf{central-angle} between two vectors in spherical coordinates is:
\begin{equation}
    \acs{central-angle}=\arccos\left(\cos\acs{ze}\cos\acs{ze'}+\sin\acs{ze}\sin\acs{ze'}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|\right).\label{gcd}
\end{equation}
For constant \acs{az}, \acs{ze}, and \acs{az'}, the value of \acs{ze'} which minimizes \acs{central-angle} can be calculated by setting:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \acs{ze'}}\arccos\left(\cos\acs{ze}\cos\acs{ze'}+\sin\acs{ze}\sin\acs{ze'}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|\right)=0.
\end{equation}
Differentiating using the chain rule,
\begin{equation}
    \frac{-\left(-\cos\acs{ze}\sin\ac{ze'}+\sin\acs{ze}\cos\ac{ze'}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|\right)}{\sqrt{1-{\left(\cos\acs{ze}\cos\acs{ze'}+\sin\acs{ze}\sin\acs{ze'}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|\right)}^2}}=0.\label{eq:derivative}
\end{equation}
Solving\footnote{The denominator of Equation~\eqref{eq:derivative} is zero only when \acs{central-angle} is equal to \ang{0} (or \ang{180}, not a physical possibility here). For $\acs{central-angle}=0$, cancelling the denominator yields an undefined result; however, Equation~\eqref{eq:constraint-robust} still works because $\acs{central-angle}=0\implies\acs{az}=\acs{az'}\implies\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|=1\implies\acs{ze'}=\arctan(\tan\acs{ze})$ as desired.} for \acs{ze'}:
\begin{gather}
    -\cos\acs{ze}\sin\ac{ze'}+\sin\acs{ze}\cos\ac{ze'}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|=0,\nonumber\\
    \frac{\sin\acs{ze}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|}{\cos\acs{ze}}=\frac{\sin\ac{ze'}}{\cos\ac{ze'}},\nonumber\\
    \ac{ze'}=\arctan\left(\tan\ac{ze}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|\right).\label{eq:constraint}
\end{gather}
The calculated value of \acs{ze'} can then be substituted back into Equation~\eqref{gcd} to find \acs{central-angle}. However, one adjustment must be made to this formula to account for edge cases. Notice in Equation~\eqref{eq:constraint} that when $\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|$ is negative, so is \acs{ze'}. If we recall that \acs{ze'} ultimately describes a slope, a negative value does not make sense. In fact, this situation corresponds to an \emph{uphill} slope being a closer match with the observed topography than a downhill one, which occurs when $|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}|>\ang{90}$. These edge cases are treated by redefining negative \acs{ze'} values to zero. Thus, the equation implemented in this project is:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\ac{ze'}=\max[0,\arctan\left(\tan\ac{ze}\cos\left|\acs{az'}-\acs{az}\right|\right)].}\label{eq:constraint-robust}
\end{equation} 

\subsection{Radial Component of 3D Deformation}
Figure~\ref{geometric-radial} illustrates how to find the component of 3D deformation along the radial-axial plane. It is just the difference of two results calculated by the procedure developed in Section~\ref{3d-deform}:
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\acs{radial-deform}=\arctan\left(\tan\ac{ze}\cos\left|\acs{THETA}-\acs{az}\right|\right)-\arctan\left(\tan\ac{ze'}\cos\left|\acs{THETA}-\acs{az'}\right|\right).}
\end{equation}
Note the correction used in Equation~\eqref{eq:constraint-robust} is not desired here, since the vectors \acs{normal} and \acs{normal'} could just as easily project on either side of the $z-$axis. Also, the sign of the difference (order of terms) is significant. For example, \ang{-5} of radial deformation would represent a ``caving-in'' toward the caldera, while \ang{5} of radial deformation would be more like an outward bulge.

The same \acs{central-angle}-minimizing procedure developed in Section~\ref{3d-deform} is used here, but now both \acs{normal} and \acs{normal'} are projected onto the radial azimuth plane, and the signed difference between their projected zenith angles is calculated.}\label{geometric-radial}
